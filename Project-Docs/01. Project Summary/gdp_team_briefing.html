01Pharma distribution in 5 minutes

We are not the company that makes the drug. We are a wholesale distributor (a warehouse + logistics operation) that buys medicines from manufacturers and sells/ships them to B2B customers — pharmacies, hospitals, clinics. Think of it like an Amazon warehouse, but for medicine, under heavy regulation.

Because it's medicine, there's an EU rulebook called GDP that governs how products are stored, moved, and — crucially for us — what happens when something goes wrong and a customer complains. The whole point of our project is the complaint handling process: from the moment a customer emails us, through investigation, to the regulated decision about what to do with the affected goods, plus fixing the customer's invoice.

The two things that can happen to returned goods

This binary is the heart of everything. When goods come back, a qualified person must decide:

  • Back to saleable stock — the goods are fine, put them back in the warehouse to sell again. (Only allowed if strict conditions are met.)
  • Destruction — the goods can't be trusted, so they must be destroyed and documented. The default when in doubt, because patient safety.

Every abbreviation, decoded

GDP
Good Distribution Practice. The EU rulebook (guideline 2013/C 343/01) for how medicines are stored & distributed. Our flow must comply with it.
GMP
Good Manufacturing Practice. Same idea but for making drugs, not distributing. Out of our scope — just don't confuse it with GDP.
QA
Quality Assurance. The department/person responsible for product quality decisions. One of our four users.
RP
Responsible Person. A legally named individual at a distributor who must personally authorize whether returned goods go back to stock. By law this cannot be automated away.
B2B
Business-to-business. Our customers are pharmacies/hospitals, not individual patients.
Batch / Lot
A production run of a product, identified by a batch number. Recalls, quality issues, and stock are tracked per batch. Each batch has a quantity and a status.
Cold chain
Products (e.g. vaccines, insulin) that must stay within a temperature range the whole journey. A "temperature excursion" = it got too warm/cold.
Excursion
A temperature deviation outside the allowed range during storage/transport. May or may not ruin the product, depending on the product's tolerance.
Stability budget / MKT
How much temperature deviation a product can tolerate before it's unsafe. MKT = Mean Kinetic Temperature, the formula used to judge it.
Quarantine
A "frozen / segregated" stock status. Returned goods sit here, unsaleable, until QA/RP decides their fate. A real GDP requirement.
FEFO
First-Expiry-First-Out. Stock rotation rule: ship the soonest-to-expire units first. Where returned-to-stock goods get placed.
OOS
Out Of Specification. A product failed a quality test / doesn't meet its spec.
CAPA
Corrective And Preventive Action. The formal "fix it and stop it happening again" process triggered by serious issues.
Deviation
A logged record that something went outside normal/approved process. Often the trigger for a CAPA.
Root cause
The underlying reason a complaint happened (e.g. human error on the order, damage in transit). Must be recorded before QA decides.
Credit note
A document that reduces what the customer owes us — i.e. we refund/credit them (e.g. they returned goods or were overcharged).
Debit note
The opposite — the customer owes us more (e.g. we accidentally shipped extra and they're keeping it).
ERP
Enterprise Resource Planning system (e.g. SAP). The big business database holding sales orders, inventory, invoices. We'll mock this as a desktop app.
Sales order
The record of what the customer ordered (product, quantity, price). We pull it to compare against what they say they received.
Saga pattern
A software pattern for long transactions: every forward action has a defined "undo" (compensating) action, so you can reverse cleanly if something changes. Our override branch uses this.
HITL
Human-In-The-Loop. A point where a person must approve/decide before the process continues. We have several.
SLA
Service Level Agreement. A time deadline (e.g. "acknowledge the customer within 24h"). Drives our timers/escalations.

The three complaint types we handle

We deliberately scoped to three (and excluded counterfeit/recall, which trigger mandatory authority reporting and would balloon the build):

FamilyWhat the customer complains aboutCore question
A · Quality defectGoods are damaged, contaminated, mislabeled, or suspected substandard.Is the product still safe? (usually → destruction)
B · Cold chainShipment arrived too warm / a temperature alarm fired.Did the excursion exceed the product's tolerance?
C · Logistics errorWrong product, wrong quantity (short or surplus), wrong customer, missing docs.Is the claim valid, and is it a human error? (often just a paperwork/$ fix)

Key insight your build depends on

Families A & B are about product integrity → they always go to QA/RP for a disposition decision. Family C is often just a commercial/quantity fix → supply chain resolves it with a credit/debit and only notifies QA for metrics. Not every complaint reaches QA. That routing is a core part of our cleverness.

The one regulation that drives the logic (GDP Ch. 6.3)

Returned goods may go back to saleable stock only if ALL of these are true. If any is false — or unknown — the default is destruction. This is the decision engine:

  1. Goods in their unopened, undamaged secondary packaging, in good condition.
  2. Evidence of proper storage/transport conditions the entire time away (the hard one — "we don't know" counts as a FAIL).
  3. Acceptable remaining shelf life.
  4. Not previously returned, recalled, or suspected falsified.
  5. Examined & authorized by the RP.

Plus timing: only considered for return if back in our care within 10 days of dispatch (5 days for non-wholesaler customers like a pharmacy).

The detail that scores us points

Most teams build "if damaged → destroy." We build TRUE / FALSE / UNKNOWN for each condition, and treat UNKNOWN as failure (you can't prove safety → you destroy). That's the inspector-grade nuance that reads as real domain understanding.

02The end-to-end flow

One case per complaint moves through these stages. The same backbone serves all three families — that's what makes it a clean Maestro Case (context-driven) rather than a fixed BPMN flow.

1
Intake & triage Agent Robot Gate 1
Email arrives. An agent extracts customer/product/batch/qty/refs. A robot pulls the matching sales order from the ERP to compare. Customer Service confirms it's a real, well-classified complaint.
2
Acknowledge & completeness check
Auto-reply to the customer (SLA timer). If info is missing, send an RFI (request for information) and park the case — this demonstrates surviving interruptions.
3
Cross-case correlation Agent
Check if another open case is the mirror image (Customer X short ↔ Customer Y surplus, same dispatch). If so, link them and resolve together. (Creativity points.)
4
Investigation (family-specific) Agent Robot CS/SC
A: pull batch records. B: pull temperature logger + stability budget. C: warehouse inventory check + ERP order-vs-shipped comparison to decide if the claim is valid and whether goods need to return.
5
Lane split
Commercial lane (valid C, no goods return) → supply chain issues credit/debit, QA notified only. QA lane (A, B, or C with returns) → build the dossier.
6
Dossier + root cause Agent Required input Gate 2
Agent assembles a structured dossier scoring the 6.3 criteria TRUE/FALSE/UNKNOWN. The investigator must enter a root cause before it can proceed. QA analyst signs off that evidence is complete.
7
Policy router → two paths
Path A (standing policy says e.g. "always destroy"): the case forks — RP ratifies in one track while logistics & finance prepare (reversible only) in parallel. Path B (genuine case-by-case): RP decides first, then logistics & finance follow.
8
Disposition decision Gate 3 RP/QA
The regulated, legally-required human call: back-to-stock vs destroy, against the 6.3 checklist.
9
Physical return → batch state machine Robot
Goods return → robot adjusts batch quantity & sets status QUARANTINE in ERP. After QA: → AVAILABLE (FEFO) or → BLOCKED / awaiting destruction. Destruction is the one irreversible act and always waits for RP.
10
Follow-up + finance Gate 4 Gate 5 Robot
Logistics approves the return/redelivery plan; finance approves the credit/debit amount; a robot posts it to the ERP.
11
Closure
Notify customer, close case, update QA metrics, full audit trail retained for inspection.

The signature edge case — demo this live

On Path A, logistics/finance start work in parallel before RP ratifies. If the RP then overrides the policy: if nothing irreversible happened yet → cheap cancel & reroute. If a credit was already posted → Saga reversal (issue a reversing note, reconcile ERP) + raise a deviation. Because destruction never runs before RP sign-off, goods are never wrongly destroyed. Triggering this on camera is where the technical-execution points live.

03The four users & what they see

Design the UI around these four. In UiPath terms, each interacts through Maestro Process Apps (dashboards) and Action Center (approval tasks). Click each persona:

Customer Service Agent — "the front door"

Goal: turn a messy email into a clean, valid case fast. Owns Gate 1 (triage).

Sees: an inbox-style queue of incoming complaints, each pre-filled by the intake agent (customer, product, batch, the AI's draft classification A/B/C). They confirm or correct the classification, flag spam/misroutes, and fire RFIs when data is missing.

[ CASE INBOX ] ▸ CMP-1041 Acme Pharmacy · "box arrived crushed" · AI: Family A · ⚠ batch# missing
  [ Confirm classification ▾ ] [ Request info ] [ Mark not-a-complaint ]

UX priority: one-screen confirm; the AI did the typing, they just validate. Show the AI's confidence and what it extracted.

Supply Chain Coordinator — "the investigator & fixer"

Goal: for logistics/quantity claims, find the truth and resolve commercially. Owns the Family-C validation, the commercial lane, and Gate 4 (follow-up plan).

Sees: a case with a side-by-side Ordered vs Shipped vs Customer-claims table (pulled from ERP), a warehouse inventory-check action, and the correlation panel if a mirror case exists. Decides credit/debit/redeliver/return.

CMP-1043 · Wrong qty
Ordered: 100   Shipped: 120   Customer reports: 120 received
🔗 Linked to CMP-1044 (Beta Hospital, 80 received, ordered 100)
  Root cause: [ pick error ▾ ]  → [ Debit Acme +20 ] [ Credit Beta +20 ] [ Arrange transfer ]

UX priority: make the discrepancy obvious at a glance; surface the linked case prominently.

QA Analyst — "the gatekeeper of evidence"

Goal: ensure the case is complete & sound before the RP decides. Owns Gate 2. Also receives non-blocking notifications from the commercial lane for metrics.

Sees: the assembled dossier — complaint facts, evidence links, batch history, and the 6.3 criteria each marked ✓/✗/?. They confirm completeness or send it back for more evidence. Plus a metrics dashboard (complaints by family, root cause trends).

DOSSIER · CMP-1041 (Family A)
✓ packaging damaged (FAIL→destroy)  ? storage history unknown  ✓ shelf life ok
Root cause: transit damage  | Recommendation: DESTROY
  [ Evidence complete → send to RP ]  [ Send back for more info ]

UX priority: the criteria checklist with evidence links is the centerpiece; "?" states visually flagged.

Responsible Person — "the legal authority"

Goal: make & e-sign the regulated disposition decision. Owns Gate 3 (and Path-A ratification). This is the one decision the law says must be a human.

Sees: a focused decision screen — the recommendation, the 6.3 checklist, root cause, and two big buttons: Authorize return to stock / Order destruction. On Path A they instead Ratify / Override a policy decision. Every action is e-signed and timestamped to the audit trail.

  [ ✅ Authorize back-to-stock ]  [ 🔥 Order destruction ]  e-sign required

UX priority: minimal, unambiguous, irreversible-action confirmation, full traceability.

Why Case, not BPMN

The hackathon tip: predictable sequence → BPMN; unpredictable paths that emerge as work unfolds → Case. Our flow branches on family, validity, policy, and human overrides — the path isn't knowable up front. That's textbook Maestro Case. (You can still embed small BPMN/DMN pieces inside it for the deterministic sub-bits, which shows range.)

Scoring strategy — deliberate choices

  • Use a coding agent visibly. Build at least one agent or workflow with Claude Code through UiPath for Coding Agents and show it in the video. Pure bonus points, low effort.
  • Drive two desktop apps with a robot. The robot shuttling data ERP ↔ cockpit is the most "UiPath-native" thing we can show and grabs the desktop-app points.
  • Trigger one exception live. Don't just describe the override/compensation — stage a demo case that fires it.
  • Show a parked case resuming. Pause on an SLA timer, then resume — proves "survives interruptions."
  • Keep humans visibly in charge. Four distinct personas approving four distinct decisions = the Track-1 thesis made literal.
  • One external-framework agent (optional). e.g. a LangChain/CrewAI agent for correlation, orchestrated by Maestro — hits "blend native + external" interest and cross-platform award.

05Mock data, tools & templates to create

Everything is fabricated. Here's the full shopping list, grouped. Build these first — the demo is only as good as the seed data.

A · Mock systems to build
  • Mock ERP (desktop app, Electron recommended) — screens for: Sales Order lookup; Inventory/Batch view (product, batch#, quantity, status, expiry); Postings (create credit/debit note). The robot drives this by its UI.
  • Case Management cockpit (desktop app) — the persona-facing UI: case list, dossier view, the 5 gate approvals, 6.3 checklist, metrics dashboard. Note: the case orchestration lives in Maestro; this cockpit is the human-facing front-end the robot/agents update. You can also surface gates via Maestro Process Apps directly — decide per time budget.
B · Mock master data (the "database")
  • Products (~8–10): code, name, form, whether cold-chain (Y/N), stability/excursion budget, shelf life.
  • Batches (~20): batch#, product, quantity, status (Available/Quarantine/Blocked), expiry date, manufacture date.
  • Customers (~6): name, type (pharmacy/hospital/wholesaler — drives the 5- vs 10-day rule), address, account terms.
  • Sales orders / dispatches (~15): order#, customer, lines (product, qty ordered, qty shipped), dispatch date, carrier, dispatch reference.
  • Disposition policies (the Path-A table): product/complaint-type → "always destroy" / "case-by-case". A few rows.
  • Temperature logger files (for Family B): a couple of CSVs — one within budget, one breaching.
C · Mock cases to seed (your demo script)

Pre-build these so each demoes a distinct path:

  • CMP-A1 — Quality defect, damaged packaging → clean DESTROY (Path A policy match).
  • CMP-B1 — Cold-chain, excursion within budget → back-to-stock (Path B, all 6.3 pass).
  • CMP-B2 — Cold-chain, logger missing → UNKNOWN → destroy (shows the UNKNOWN-as-fail logic).
  • CMP-C1 — Short delivery, human pick error → commercial lane, credit, QA notified only (no QA gate).
  • CMP-C2 + CMP-C3 — Wrong-customer pair → cross-case correlation & linked resolution.
  • CMP-A2 — Path A where RP overrides after a credit posted → Saga compensation (the showpiece).
  • CMP-X — Missing batch# email → RFI → parked → resumes (interruption survival).
D · Email templates (mock inbound + outbound)
  • Inbound complaint emails (one per case above) — varied phrasing, some with attachments, one ambiguous/spam.
  • Outbound: acknowledgement, RFI (request for info), resolution notice, destruction confirmation.
E · Document templates the flow generates
  • Complaint dossier (the QA/RP review pack) — facts, evidence, 6.3 checklist, root cause, recommendation.
  • Credit note / Debit note — posted to mock ERP.
  • Certificate of destruction.
  • Audit trail / case timeline export (for the "inspection-ready" story).
  • Deviation/CAPA record (stub) — raised on the override case.
F · The decision rules (DMN tables)
  • 6.3 disposition table: inputs = packaging ok?, storage proven?, shelf life ok?, prior return/recall?, within time window? → output = eligible-for-stock / destroy / unknown→destroy.
  • Family classifier table (or agent): keywords/fields → A/B/C + severity.
  • Policy router table: product + complaint type → Path A / Path B.
  • Finance direction table: scenario → credit / debit / none / net-zero.

06Suggested build order

  1. Seed the mock master data (B) — nothing works without it.
  2. Build the two desktop apps (A) with the screens listed; hard-code reads from the seed data.
  3. Stand up the Maestro Case skeleton: stages 1–11 as a happy-path Case with the case/stage manager agents.
  4. Wire the 4 DMN tables (F) for classification, 6.3, policy, finance.
  5. Build the Agent Builder agents: intake/extraction, correlation, dossier writer.
  6. Build the RPA robots that drive the two desktop apps (pull SO, set batch status, post notes).
  7. Add the 5 Action Center gates + the persona Process Apps.
  8. Add timers/SLA + the RFI parked-case path.
  9. Add Path A fork + JOIN + Saga compensation handlers.
  10. Seed the demo cases (C) and rehearse the 5-minute script.
  11. Build one piece with Claude Code on camera for the bonus.

Time-budget honesty

If you run short: the must-haves for a coherent demo are stages 1, 4, 6, 8, 9, 10 (one happy path A or B end-to-end) + one desktop app + 6.3 DMN + at least two HITL gates. The override/Saga case and correlation are high-value extras — build them only once the spine works. Don't let the showpiece sink the basics.
